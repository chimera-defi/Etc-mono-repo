## What to Expect

### Meteor Characteristics
- **Color**: Predominantly white/yellow, with some green and blue
- **Speed**: Medium velocity (35 km/sec) - easier to see than fast meteors
- **Brightness**: Range from faint to very bright fireballs
- **Trains**: Some may leave persistent glowing trails for 1-2 seconds
- **Fireballs**: Expect several bright fireballs throughout the night

### Realistic Expectations for Bolivia

Under **perfect conditions** (dark sky, no clouds, no light pollution):
- **60-100 meteors per hour** at peak (one every 36-60 seconds on average)

Under **typical conditions** (some light pollution, occasional clouds):
- **30-50 meteors per hour** (one every 1-2 minutes)

**Note**: Meteors come in bursts - you might see 5 in a minute, then none for 10 minutes. Be patient!

## What Makes the Geminids Special

### Unique Origin: An Asteroid, Not a Comet!

The Geminids are one of the only major meteor showers caused by an **asteroid** rather than a comet:

- **Parent Object**: Asteroid **3200 Phaethon**
- Phaethon is a "rock comet" - it sheds particles when heated by the Sun
- Discovered in 1983 by the IRAS satellite
- Orbital period: 1.4 years
- Gets closer to the Sun than any other named asteroid

### Why Geminids Are the "King of Meteor Showers"

1. **Reliability**: Peak every year without fail
2. **High rates**: Up to 150 meteors per hour at peak
3. **Visible all night**: Radiant rises around 7-8 PM
4. **Bright meteors**: Many visible even with light pollution
5. **Multi-colored**: Variety of colors due to mineral composition
6. **Growing stronger**: Rate has been increasing over decades

## Viewing Tips

### Essential Preparation

1. **Find a dark location**
   - Get away from city lights
   - Ideal: Rural areas, mountains, altiplano
   - Minimum: Parks, sports fields with minimal lighting

2. **Bring comfort gear**
   - Blanket or reclining lawn chair
   - Warm clothing (even tropical Bolivia gets cool at night)
   - Hot beverages
   - Snacks

3. **Avoid**
   - Looking at phone screens (ruins night vision)
   - White flashlights (use red light if needed)
   - Telescopes or binoculars (naked eye is best for meteors)

## The Science: Why December 14?

Earth crosses through the debris trail of asteroid 3200 Phaethon every year in mid-December. The geometry works out so that:

- **Dec 4**: Shower begins (Earth enters debris stream)
- **Dec 13-14**: Peak (densest part of debris stream)
- **Dec 17**: Shower ends (Earth exits debris stream)

The radiant (where meteors appear to originate) is in the constellation Gemini, giving the shower its name.
